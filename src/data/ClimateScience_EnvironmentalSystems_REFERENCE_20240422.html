Global Climate Science and Environmental Systems: A Research Reference

This reference covers the physical science of climate change, major research institutions, international agreements, prominent scientists, observed impacts, and mitigation technologies.

Section 1: Climate System Fundamentals

The Carbon Cycle

The global carbon cycle describes the exchange of carbon among four major reservoirs: the atmosphere, the terrestrial biosphere, the ocean, and the lithosphere. As of 2024, the atmospheric CO2 concentration has reached 422 parts per million (ppm), compared to 280 ppm in pre-industrial times (1750). The atmosphere currently holds approximately 880 GtC (gigatons of carbon). The terrestrial biosphere stores roughly 2,300 GtC in vegetation, soils, and permafrost. The ocean is the largest active reservoir, containing approximately 38,000 GtC. Human activities emit approximately 10 GtC per year through fossil fuel combustion and 1.6 GtC per year through land use change. Natural land and ocean sinks absorb approximately 5.5 GtC per year combined, resulting in a net accumulation of about 5 GtC annually in the atmosphere.

Greenhouse Gases and Radiative Forcing

Greenhouse gases absorb and re-emit infrared radiation, trapping heat in the atmosphere. Carbon dioxide (CO2) is the primary anthropogenic greenhouse gas with a Global Warming Potential (GWP) of 1 (baseline, 100-year horizon) and an effective atmospheric lifetime of 300–1,000 years due to chemical buffering by the ocean and terrestrial systems. Methane (CH4) has a GWP100 of 28–34 and a lifetime of approximately 12 years. Its atmospheric concentration has risen from 722 ppb pre‑industrial to 1,923 ppb in 2023. Sources include natural gas systems, livestock enteric fermentation, rice paddies, landfills, and wetlands. Nitrous oxide (N2O) has a GWP100 of 273 and a 109‑year lifetime, emitted primarily from agricultural soils, livestock manure, and industrial processes. Hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) used in refrigeration and air conditioning have GWPs from 12 to 14,800. The Kigali Amendment to the Montreal Protocol (2016) phases down HFC production and consumption. Total anthropogenic radiative forcing in 2019 was 2.72 W/m² relative to 1750, with CO2 contributing 1.82 W/m².

Paleoclimatology and Ice Core Evidence

Ice cores drilled from Antarctica (Dome C, Vostok) and Greenland (GRIP, NGRIP) provide records of past climate extending 800,000 years. Trapped air bubbles preserve ancient atmosphere composition. CO2 and temperature are tightly correlated across eight glacial-interglacial cycles. During ice ages, CO2 was approximately 180 ppm; during warm interglacials, approximately 280 ppm. The current 422 ppm exceeds any level in the ice core record. The EPICA Dome C core (Antarctica) shows temperature fluctuations of 8–10°C between glacials and interglacials in Antarctica. Dendrochronology (tree rings), coral skeleton isotopes, lake sediments, and speleothems (cave formations) complement ice core records for the Holocene period.

Climate Feedbacks and Sensitivity

Climate feedbacks amplify or dampen the initial warming from radiative forcing. Positive feedbacks that amplify warming include: water vapor feedback (largest positive feedback, amplifying by factor of ~2×), ice-albedo feedback (retreating ice and snow exposes darker ocean and land), and permafrost carbon feedback (thawing permafrost releases stored CO2 and CH4). Negative feedbacks include increased thermal emission (Planck response) and increased cloud coverage. Equilibrium Climate Sensitivity (ECS) is the long-term temperature increase for a doubling of CO2. The IPCC AR6 assessed ECS as 2.5–4°C with a best estimate of 3°C. Transient Climate Response (TCR) measures warming at the time of CO2 doubling under a gradual increase and is assessed at 1.2–2.4°C.

Section 2: International Climate Governance

UNFCCC and Conference of the Parties (COP)

The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) was adopted at the Earth Summit in Rio de Janeiro in 1992 and entered into force in 1994. It has near-universal membership with 198 parties. The Convention's supreme decision-making body is the Conference of the Parties (COP), which meets annually. Notable COP sessions include: COP3 Kyoto (1997) producing the Kyoto Protocol; COP15 Copenhagen (2009) failing to produce a binding agreement; COP21 Paris (2015) producing the Paris Agreement; COP26 Glasgow (2021) with commitments on methane, coal phase-out, and deforestation; and COP28 Dubai (2023) producing the first global stocktake calling for a transition away from fossil fuels.

The Paris Agreement (2015)

The Paris Agreement was adopted at COP21 on December 12, 2015 and entered into force November 4, 2016 after ratification by 55 parties representing 55% of global emissions. It is a legally binding international treaty under the UNFCCC umbrella. Its temperature goal is to hold warming well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels and to pursue efforts to limit warming to 1.5°C. The Agreement operates through Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs): voluntary national climate action plans submitted every five years with a ratcheting mechanism requiring progressively more ambitious targets. The first Global Stocktake (2023) found current NDCs insufficient to meet the 1.5°C goal, with projected warming of 2.5–2.9°C under current policies. Climate finance of $100 billion per year from developed to developing nations was pledged at Copenhagen but not fulfilled; COP29 Baku (2024) negotiated a new finance goal.

The Kyoto Protocol (1997)

The Kyoto Protocol operationalized the UNFCCC by committing industrialized (Annex I) nations to legally binding emissions reduction targets. The first commitment period (2008–2012) required Annex I parties to reduce GHG emissions by an average of 5.2% below 1990 levels. Market mechanisms included: the Clean Development Mechanism (CDM) allowing developed countries to fund emission-reduction projects in developing countries for credits; Joint Implementation (JI) between Annex I countries; and International Emissions Trading (IET). The US signed but the Senate never ratified. Canada withdrew in 2011. The Doha Amendment (2012) extended the protocol through 2020 with a -18% target for the second commitment period but did not enter into force before the Paris Agreement superseded it.

Section 4: Prominent Climate Scientists

James Hansen

James Hansen served as Director of NASA GISS from 1981 to 2013 and is now Adjunct Professor at Columbia University. In June 1988 he testified before the US Senate that global warming was already being detected with high confidence, a landmark moment in climate policy. Hansen developed GISS ModelE and contributed to the first quantitative assessments of the enhanced greenhouse effect. He coined the term "Venus syndrome" for runaway greenhouse scenarios. In 1988 he described three scenarios for future warming based on high, medium, and low emissions; actual observed warming closely tracks his intermediate scenario. His 2023 paper "Global warming in the pipeline" projected 1.5°C crossed in the early 2030s and 2°C by 2050. Hansen is also known for climate advocacy including civil disobedience at coal plant protests and challenging his own agency's communications restrictions.

Syukuro (Suki) Manabe (Nobel Prize 2021)

Syukuro Manabe received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2021 (shared with Klaus Hasselmann and Giorgio Parisi) for his contributions to the physical modeling of Earth's climate. Manabe and Richard Wetherald published the foundational 1967 paper establishing the first quantitative estimate of CO2-induced warming using a radiative-convective model, predicting approximately 2°C warming for CO2 doubling. Manabe later developed the first coupled ocean-atmosphere climate model at NOAA GFDL. He is a Senior Meteorologist at Princeton University. His models demonstrated that the stratosphere cools while the troposphere warms as a consequence of CO2 increase, a fingerprint pattern later confirmed observationally.

Klaus Hasselmann (Nobel Prize 2021)

Klaus Hasselmann received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2021 for the physical modeling of Earth's climate and for quantifying variability and reliably predicting global warming. He developed the statistical framework for detecting climate change signals in the presence of natural variability (optimal fingerprinting), providing the rigorous methodology for attributing observed temperature trends to anthropogenic forcing. He founded the Max Planck Institute for Meteorology in Hamburg. Hasselmann also developed stochastic climate models showing how natural climate variability arises from weather-scale fluctuations.

Michael E. Mann

Michael Mann is Distinguished Professor at the University of Pennsylvania. He is best known for the "hockey stick" paleoclimate temperature reconstruction (1998–1999) co-authored with Raymond Bradley and Malcolm Hughes, published in Nature and Geophysical Research Letters. The reconstruction used proxy data (tree rings, ice cores, coral, historical records) to show Northern Hemisphere temperatures were relatively stable for 900 years before rising sharply in the 20th century. The research became a focal point of political and legal attacks on climate science; Mann won all resulting legal cases. He received the Tyler Prize for Environmental Achievement (2019) and authored "The Hockey Stick and the Climate Wars" (2012).

Katharine Hayhoe

Katharine Hayhoe is Chief Scientist at The Nature Conservancy and Paul Whitfield Horn Distinguished Professor at Texas Tech University. She is a Canadian-born atmospheric scientist known for her work on regional climate projections and climate communication to diverse audiences including faith communities and politically conservative groups. She leads the Climate Science Center at Texas Tech. Hayhoe was named one of TIME's 100 Most Influential People (2014), received the UN Champion of the Earth Award (2019), and was appointed to the Federal Advisory Committee for the Fifth National Climate Assessment. She authored "Saving Us: A Climate Scientist's Case for Hope and Healing in a Divided World" (2021).

Section 5: Climate Tipping Points

Tipping points are thresholds in the climate system beyond which self-reinforcing dynamics can drive further change independent of additional forcing. The tipping elements framework was introduced by Lenton et al. (2008) in PNAS and updated in 2022 in Science. Key tipping elements include the West Antarctic Ice Sheet (WAIS), estimated threshold 1.5°C (sea level contribution 3.3m on millennial timescales); the Greenland Ice Sheet, threshold 1.5°C (sea level contribution 7m over centuries to millennia); the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) collapse, threshold 1.8–4°C (disrupts European climate, monsoons, tropical storms); the Amazon rainforest tipping point from combined deforestation and drought at ~20–25% deforestation (current ~17%); the boreal permafrost abrupt thaw adding 0.3–0.4°C equivalent warming; and tropical coral reef die-offs above 1.5°C. The 2022 Science paper identified 16 tipping elements and concluded that multiple tipping points may be interacting, creating "cascades" that lower the threshold for each other.

Section 6: Mitigation Technologies

Renewable Energy Systems

Solar photovoltaic (PV) module costs fell 90% between 2010 and 2023 from $4/W to $0.20/W, making it the cheapest source of electricity in history across most of the world. Global solar capacity reached 1.6 TW in 2023 after adding a record 340 GW. Wind power (onshore and offshore combined) reached 1.0 TW in 2023. The IEA projects solar and wind must reach 11 TW by 2030 to stay on a Net Zero by 2050 trajectory. Green hydrogen produced via proton exchange membrane (PEM) electrolysis powered by renewables is projected to decarbonize steel production, shipping, and aviation. The current levelized cost of green hydrogen is $4–7/kg, targeting $1–2/kg by 2030. Battery storage costs (lithium iron phosphate, LFP) fell from $1,200/kWh in 2010 to $100/kWh in 2023, enabling grid-scale storage.

Carbon Capture and Removal (CDR)

The IPCC AR6 concludes that CDR is necessary under all modeled pathways to limit warming below 2°C. CDR approaches span biological, geochemical, and technological methods. Bioenergy with Carbon Capture and Storage (BECCS) grows biomass (which sequesters CO2), burns it for energy, and captures the flue gas CO2 for geological storage. Direct Air Capture (DAC) uses chemical sorbents or liquid solvents to extract CO2 directly from ambient air; Climeworks operates the world's largest DAC plant (Mammoth, Iceland, 36,000 tCO2/yr capacity). Current DAC costs are $400–1,000/tCO2 with targets of $100–150/tCO2 by 2035. Enhanced rock weathering (ERW) accelerates natural silicate mineral dissolution to sequester carbon in soils and oceans. Ocean alkalinity enhancement (OAE) adds alkaline minerals to seawater to increase CO2 absorption capacity. Afforestation and reforestation can sequester 1.5–3 GtCO2/year globally but face land-use competition with food production and biodiversity.

Nuclear Energy

Nuclear power provides approximately 10% of global electricity with near-zero lifecycle GHG emissions (5–15 gCO2eq/kWh, comparable to wind and solar). As of 2024, 440 nuclear reactors are operating globally (Net 413 GW capacity). France generates 71% of its electricity from nuclear. The IEA Net Zero scenario requires nuclear capacity to double to 812 GW by 2050. Small Modular Reactors (SMRs, <300 MW) are being developed by NuScale (USA, NRC certified 2023), Rolls-Royce (UK), X-energy (USA), and GE Hitachi (BWRX-300). Generation IV reactor designs include sodium-cooled fast reactors (Natrium, TerraPower), molten salt reactors (Terrestrial Energy), and gas-cooled reactors offering inherent safety and waste reduction. Nuclear fusion remains a long-term prospect: ITER (35-nation project, construction completion 2025) targets first plasma; Commonwealth Fusion Systems (CFS) targets a demonstration plant (SPARC) in the late 2020s using high-temperature superconducting magnets.
